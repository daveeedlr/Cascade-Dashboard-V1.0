Storyboard 04 —
Executive · Francisco

The owner. He does not approve purchase orders or configure connectors — he asks whether the business is working, and he is the only person who can tell the agent when it is wrong. This board is about judgement, not tasks: the 360 view he asked for, the weekly narrative, and the one request we deliberately said no to.

Every board so far ends at a decision someone makes inside a workflow. Francisco sits above all of them. His questions are the ones from the discovery call — where are the sellers, what happened on the visit, why are we not selling the other lines, what is each store really worth to us. The design problem is restraint: an executive screen that answers those without turning into a wall of charts nobody reads. And one frame here says no to something he explicitly asked for, which is a design decision as much as a commercial one.

Frame note

This is the "no" branch of the writes-outside gate in the architecture diagram — a read-only question, answered instantly, no approval needed.

  • Francisco will never build a report. He'll ask a question in the words he already uses.
  • The answer ends in a number he can act on, not a table he has to interpret.
  • Cites its sources, same as the admin board. Same component, different language.
  • Top bar says "solo lectura" — he can't accidentally change anything.

Frame note

The report that made John say the straw man was the sales narrative. Here it's a product surface — and it commits to an opinion, then asks to be corrected.

  • "Lo que no estoy seguro" is the most important card. An agent that flags its own weak spots gets trusted with the strong ones.
  • Four sentences, not four pages. Francisco reads this on a phone.
  • The correction button feeds the same knowledge model as Storyboard 03 — one loop, two entry points.

Lo que sí puedo hacer: sus vendedores mencionan a la competencia en los reportes de visita. Puedo contar esas menciones y decirle en qué zonas aparecen más — pero eso es un módulo aparte, no está en estos 3 meses.

Frame note

The proposal names competitor intelligence as a scope guard. This frame is what that looks like in the product instead of in a contract nobody re-reads.

  • The agent refuses to guess and says why — "a made-up number here would cost you money."
  • It offers the nearest thing it can honestly do, and prices it as separate work. Scope control as an upsell path.
  • Out-of-scope is visible and off, never silently missing. Same pattern as the GL write in Storyboard 02.
  • The request is logged — that's the roadmap writing itself from real demand.

What this board settles

The executive product is judgement, not features. Francisco gets four numbers, one narrative with an opinion attached, and a straight answer when the system can't help. The two frames that matter most are the ones where the agent admits something: "de lo que no estoy seguro" and "eso no está dentro de lo que puedo ver hoy." Those are what make the confident answers believable, and they're the reason he'll keep paying after month three.
