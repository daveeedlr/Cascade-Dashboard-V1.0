The storyboards show what each user sees. This shows what the system does underneath all of them. Read it top to bottom: data comes in through the connector layer, the agent reads and reasons, and then it hits a series of gates. Every diamond is a decision with exactly two exits. Follow the green line and you reach a real action; every red line stops somewhere safe and says why. The tag on each gate points at the storyboard frame that draws it.

Process
Something the system does automatically.
Decision
A gate. Two exits only — yes or no.
Human gate
A person must actively approve.
Completed
An end state that did something.
Stopped
An end state that safely did nothing.

01No path skips the human

Trace every green line. There is no route from a connected system to a write that does not pass the approval gate. That is the safety claim, and the diagram is the proof.

02Every "no" lands somewhere

Seven gates, seven red exits — and not one of them is a dead end. Each stops safely, says why, and names who can unblock it.

03Reading is cheap, writing is expensive

The "writes to an outside system?" gate is the fork that matters. Answer no and you get an instant answer. Answer yes and you inherit four more gates.

04Being told no is how it learns

Both purple loops end at the knowledge model. A rejection is not a failure — it is the input that makes the next run better, and it is what we actually sell.

How to use this with a client

This is the one page to put in front of a buyer who asks "what stops it doing something stupid?" It answers in their language without a word of architecture jargon:

  • Point at the gates. Seven of them, each with a person or a rule behind it.
  • Point at the green line. It never reaches the bottom without a human saying yes.
  • Point at the purple loops. That's the part that compounds — the system gets more useful the longer they own it.
  • Point at "Blocked — out of scope." That's the answer to "could it touch the general ledger?" No, not unless you turn it on.
